Vehicle logo recognition system based on convolutional networks
with a pre-training strategy


Abstract

Since the vehicle logo is the clearest indicator of the vehicle manufacturer, vehicle logo recognition (VLR) always plays a crucial role in intelligent transportation systems (ITS). VLR is always useful to provide car ownership statistics to governments and businesses. In addition, manufacturer recognition can assist in vehicle identification, e.g. vehicle license plates are often replaced in stolen cars.

The project proposed an efficient vehicle logo recognition by convolutional neural networks with PCA based pre-training strategy. The contributions of the proposed work can be summarized as:
(1) a PCA based pre-training strategy has been suggested for the CNN logo recognition model to dramatically reduce the computational cost of training procedures, thus ensuring that the proposed models satisfy the requirement of logo recognition for efficient training procedures, since the training samples are updated frequently;
(2) rather than hand crafted features, the proposed system automatically extracts features which are robust enough to provide a satisfactory classification accuracy with various outdoor imaging situations;
(3) a coarse segmentation approach is proposed based on a very simple assumption, so precise logo detection is no longer required.

Supplemental issues

Part I

As for the robustness against combined distortions, another experiment is proposed as follows as a simulation of some extreme cases. A set of image samples (2,000) are generated with all the tested combined distortions (combinations of various noises, illuminations and rotations), and two subsets (subset F and G) can be generated with size of 1,000 and 1,000. This aims to make sure that the training samples and testing samples are not overlap. Two classification steps are proposed in the validation. In the Step 1, training samples are the same as in the experiments section, and subset F is employed as testing samples in the recognition. And then in Step 2, the testing samples are included into the training samples with correct labels by replacing 1,000 random selected samples, thus generating the training data in next step. Subset G is applied as testing data in the second step. All the testing data in two steps are combined distorted images. Details of each step and classification accuracy are recorded as follows. It can be observed that the accuracy decrease to 88.2% when there is no combined distorted images in the training data; however, it increases to 99.2% with online updated training data.
